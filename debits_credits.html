Step 1: The Foundations of Financial Language

Before touching the words Debit or Credit, you must understand the primary architecture of every business on Earth: The Accounting Equation.

Think of it as a physical law of conservation. A business cannot magically create assets (like cash or vehicles) out of thin air. They must be financed either through borrowing money from lenders (Liabilities) or investments and accumulated earnings from the business owners (Equity).

Every single transaction in a business keeps this scale perfectly balanced. If a business buys a $10,000 truck by taking out a bank loan, Assets go up by $10,000, and Liabilities go up by $10,000. Balance is retained!

Step 2: Debits & Credits are NOT "Plus & Minus"

The most common hurdle for new students is assuming "Debit means good/plus" and "Credit means bad/minus" because of bank statement terminology. Erase that from your mind!

The Golden Definition

Debit (Dr.) simply means LEFT SIDE.
Credit (Cr.) simply means RIGHT SIDE.

Whether a debit or credit increases or decreases an account balance depends entirely on the type of account it is. To memorize this instantly, we use the legendary D-E-A-L-E-R method:

D
Dividends
Increases on Dr.
E
Expenses
Increases on Dr.
A
Assets
Increases on Dr.
L
Liabilities
Increases on Cr.
E
Equity
Increases on Cr.
R
Revenue
Increases on Cr.

The "DEA" Group (Left Side)

Dividends, Expenses, Assets increase with a Debit (Left side entry) and decrease with a Credit (Right side entry).

The "LER" Group (Right Side)

Liabilities, Equity, Revenue increase with a Credit (Right side entry) and decrease with a Debit (Left side entry).

Step 4: The Golden Rule of Double-Entry

In double-entry accounting, every transaction must balance. If you record an entry with a total of $5,000 in Debits, you *MUST* record a total of $5,000 in Credits. The balance scale cannot tilt!
